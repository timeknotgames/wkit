# Land Acknowledgement & Indigenous Data Sovereignty Statement

## For the Peoples of the Tahoma Watershed and All Our Relations

---

### The Land Beneath the Code

This project is built in the shadow of **Tahoma** — known to settlers as Mount Rainier — the great volcanic peak that the Puyallup people call *Tacobud* ("Mother of Waters"), whose glacial meltwater feeds the rivers, the Sound, and all living things downstream.

We acknowledge that this work takes place on the ancestral, traditional, and contemporary lands of the **Coast Salish peoples** — specifically the territories of the:

- **Puyallup Tribe of Indians** (*Spuyaləpabš*) — whose reservation and ceded lands encompass the city of Tacoma and the lower Puyallup River watershed
- **Nisqually Indian Tribe** — stewards of the Nisqually River watershed flowing from Tahoma's southern glaciers
- **Muckleshoot Indian Tribe** — whose territory includes the White and Green River valleys east of Tacoma
- **Squaxin Island Tribe** — People of the Water, stewards of the seven inlets of southern Puget Sound
- **Steilacoom Tribe** — the first peoples of the prairies and shores between Tacoma and Olympia
- **Duwamish Tribal Organization** — whose unceded territory spans the greater Puget Sound lowlands

These nations are signatories to the **Medicine Creek Treaty of 1854** and the **Point Elliott Treaty of 1855**, through which the United States government secured vast cessions of land in exchange for reserved rights — including fishing, hunting, and gathering rights — that remain legally binding and continue to be exercised and defended today.

**These are not historical peoples. They are here. They govern. They fish. They teach. They lead.**

### The Wider Circle: Coast Salish and Beyond

The Coast Salish world extends from the shores of what is now called British Columbia through the San Juan Islands, the Salish Sea, Puget Sound, and the Olympic Peninsula. Dozens of nations share linguistic, cultural, and kinship ties across these waters — among them the **Lummi, Tulalip, Snoqualmie, Stillaguamish, Swinomish, Suquamish, Makah, Quinault, Chehalis,** and many more. The annual **Tribal Canoe Journey** — revived over the past 35 years — carries these relationships forward across the waters in cedar canoes, reaffirming connections that predate every colonial boundary by millennia.

We acknowledge all the **First Peoples of Turtle Island** — from the salmon peoples of the Pacific Northwest to the mound builders of the Mississippi, from the Haudenosaunee Confederacy to the Diné and Pueblo peoples of the Southwest, from the Anishinaabe of the Great Lakes to the Inuit navigators of the Arctic — each carrying knowledge systems that sustained their lands since time immemorial.

### The Wayfinding Cousins

The author of this project, who grew up watching Tahoma's silhouette from the streets of Tacoma, had the honor of learning from the **Hawaiian Islands** and the Polynesian wayfinding traditions that navigate by stars, waves, wind, and the intelligence of the ocean itself.

**Mau Piailug** of Satawal, the master navigator who taught Nainoa Thompson to read the Hawaiian Star Compass; the **Hōkūleʻa**, which sailed 2,500 miles from Hawaiʻi to Tahiti in 1976 without instruments; the **Polynesian Voyaging Society**, which completed a Worldwide Voyage visiting 150 ports across 18 nations — these traditions demonstrate that Indigenous science is not a metaphor. It is navigation. It is astronomy. It is the accumulated intelligence of peoples who crossed the largest ocean on Earth using knowledge encoded in story, song, and the body's own senses.

The Hawaiian word ***kuleana*** — which means both rights and responsibilities, inseparable — is the ethical foundation of this project. We do not take data from the land without asking what we owe in return.

### To the Ancient Roots and Star Futures

Many Indigenous cosmologies trace origin to the stars. The Hawaiian people speak of ancestral connections to the **Pleiades** (*Makaliʻi*), whose rising marks the beginning of the harvest season. The Māori know them as **Matariki** — the New Year. The Lakota see the same cluster as a sacred lodge. The Puyallup and their Salish relatives carry their own star knowledge, woven into seasonal rounds and ceremony.

We acknowledge that **the minerals powering the servers running this code were extracted from someone's homeland** — coltan from the Congo, lithium from Chile, rare earths from China, copper from Arizona's sacred Oak Flat. The supply chain that delivers a Minecraft server to a child's screen passes through Indigenous territories at every stage. This project exists to make that circuit visible — not as abstraction, but as geography you can walk.

We build toward a future where the children who inherit this Earth — of all nations, all colors, all cosmologies — have tools to see their world clearly: who lived here first, what was taken, what remains, what can be restored. A future where **Indigenous Futurism** is not a genre but a design methodology. Where the star peoples' children and the salmon peoples' children and the children of every diaspora build together in worlds both digital and physical.

### Indigenous Data Sovereignty in This Project

WKit generates virtual representations of real Indigenous territories. This carries obligations:

#### OCAP Principles (First Nations Information Governance Centre)
- **Ownership:** Indigenous nations own cultural knowledge, data, and information about their territories
- **Control:** Authority over all data management and research processes
- **Access:** Rights to their own data regardless of where it is stored
- **Possession:** Physical control of data

#### CARE Principles (Global Indigenous Data Alliance)
- **Collective Benefit:** Data ecosystems must enable Indigenous peoples to derive benefit
- **Authority to Control:** Indigenous peoples' rights over their data must be recognized
- **Responsibility:** Those working with data must build relationships based on respect and reciprocity
- **Ethics:** Indigenous peoples' rights and wellbeing are the primary concern

#### Our Commitments

1. **What we DO NOT map without permission:** Sacred sites, ceremonial grounds, medicine gathering locations, culturally restricted knowledge. The void in the map is itself data — it says *"here is knowledge that exists but is not ours to display."*

2. **What we DO map with attribution:** Territory boundaries (via Native Land Digital, CC BY-NC-SA), treaty areas, publicly available cultural information — always with clear sourcing and the understanding that these maps are approximations, not legal definitions.

3. **What we build toward:** Community-controlled data layers where Indigenous nations manage their own information within the engine, determining what is visible, to whom, and under what conditions.

4. **Revenue and reciprocity:** As this project generates any economic value, we commit to directing resources back to Indigenous education programs, tribal sovereignty initiatives, and land-back efforts in the territories we represent.

5. **Right of reply:** Indigenous communities have the standing right to request corrections, additions, or removals of any data representing their territories in this engine.

### Closing

> *The land remembers what we take from it. The water carries our choices downstream. The stars watch whether we honor what was given.*

This project is a tool. Tools are not neutral — they serve the hands that hold them and the intentions that guide them. We offer WKit as a **convivial tool** in the spirit of Ivan Illich: one that amplifies the capacity of communities to understand, govern, and heal the places they call home.

We build in the shadow of Tahoma, with gratitude for the peoples who have tended this watershed since the glaciers retreated and the salmon first returned. We build with the wayfinding lessons of cousins who crossed the Pacific by starlight. We build for the children — all of them, everywhere — who will inherit what we leave behind.

**Mahalo. Háʔ kʷ(i).** *Thank you, in Hawaiian and in Lushootseed.*
